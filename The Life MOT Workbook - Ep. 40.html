Part One

Discover

Before you can fix anything, you need to see it clearly. This part runs your full Life MOT — a structured honest audit across every area that matters. No aspiration. Only what is actually true today.

1
The Annual Audit — Your Full Life MOT
Seven areas. One honest score. No rounding up.

This is the core of the Life MOT. For each area below, score where you actually are — not where you intend to be, not where you were six months ago, and not where you hope to be. The score reflects today. Then select your verdict: Pass, Advisory, or Major Fault. Use the notes column to name the specific thing — one sentence is enough.

Scoring Guide

1–3: Critical — significant breakdown or neglect  |  4–5: Struggling — noticeable problems, energy being spent managing  |  6–7: Functional — getting by, but not where you want to be  |  8–9: Strong — genuinely good, minor things to watch  |  10: Excellent — no faults, full confidence

Verdict Key

Pass — Roadworthy. No action required now.  |  Advisory — Worth watching. Schedule attention.  |  Major Fault — Must be addressed. Cannot drive indefinitely on this.

The Managing vs Fixing Distinction

Managing a fault is not the same as fixing it. Managing keeps you moving. Fixing makes you roadworthy. Everything in your Fault Log is something you have been managing. This worksheet is where you begin fixing.

The Cost Count
What has managing these faults actually cost you?

This is the exercise most people skip — because counting the cost is uncomfortable. But it is also the exercise that makes everything else in this worksheet real. A fault you can manage without counting its cost will always feel manageable. Count it. Then decide.

🎯 Coaching Moment — Answer This Honestly

Pick your primary Major Fault from the log above. Now count what managing it has cost you — not in theory, but in your actual life over the time you have been carrying it.

In your energy, your relationships, your confidence, your ability to show up fully. Not what you think — what do you feel when you actually count?
The Cost to Others
What Will This Cost You in the Next 12 Months If Nothing Changes?
If This Is Uncomfortable — That Is the Point

The discomfort of counting the cost is always less than the actual cost of continuing to carry it. The fault you can manage today will always be more expensive tomorrow. What you felt writing those answers is data. Use it.

Part Two

Define

You have named the faults and counted the cost. Now we go deeper — tracing why the fault stayed unaddressed, and building your personal Life MOT system so it cannot happen the same way again.

Why the Fault Stayed — Root Tracing
Managing is always active. What was the management protecting?

A fault that goes unaddressed for months or years is never just negligence. There is always something the not-looking is protecting. A verdict you were not ready for. An action you did not feel equipped to take. A truth that would require a change you were not prepared to make. This exercise traces the root of the delay — not to assign blame, but to understand the pattern well enough to interrupt it.

🎯 Root Tracing — Three Questions

When you first noticed this fault — what did you tell yourself about why it was not urgent yet?

Not the real reason. The story you used. "I'll deal with it when..." — what came after that?

What would have had to be true for you to have addressed it sooner?

What conditions, resources, or internal permission did you believe you needed that you do not actually need?

What has staying with the unaddressed fault given you — even a small benefit, even something you are not proud of?

This is the hardest question. Staying is always doing something for you. Comfort. Control. Deferral. Safety. Name it honestly.">
The Pattern — Is This the First Time?
This Is Not About Judgment

The reason a fault stays unaddressed is almost always rational at the time it begins. You were protecting something. The work is not to shame that — it is to see it clearly enough that you stop letting that protection cost you more than the thing it was protecting.

The Identity Distinction
Separating the fault from who you actually are.

One of the reasons faults go unaddressed is that we quietly begin to identify with them. The managed fault becomes part of how we describe ourselves — "I have always been bad with money" or "this is just how my relationship is" or "I have never been great at looking after my health." The fault stops being something you have and starts being something you are. This exercise makes the separation.

What the Fault Is Not
What the Evidence Shows
Who Are You When You Are Roadworthy in This Area?
The Most Important Distinction

The fault is something the car has. It is not what the car is. A vehicle with a major fault is still the same vehicle — capable of the same journeys, with the same engine, the same build. The fault does not change what it is built to do. It only changes whether it is currently roadworthy enough to do it. The same is true of you.

Part Three

Pressure Test

A system that only works when life is calm is not a system — it is an intention. This part tests your Life MOT framework against the moments when you will most want to skip it.

Scenario One — When Life Gets Busy
The most common reason people skip their own MOT.

It is the week of your Annual Life MOT. You have had a brutal month. A major work deadline has just landed. A family situation has taken up unexpected time and energy. The calendar is full and the date you set now feels like the wrong moment. The pull to defer it — just this once — is strong.

What Will You Tell Yourself in That Moment?
What Is the Counter-Argument You Need to Have Ready?
The Minimum Version — If You Cannot Do the Full MOT, What Is the Non-Negotiable Check?
The Pressure Test Principle

The government's MOT does not move because the driver is busy. The consequence enforces the behaviour regardless of circumstances. In the absence of that external consequence, you need a pre-decided response to your own most likely deferral. Write it now. Have it ready.

Scenario Two — When the Fault Feels Too Big
What happens when the MOT reveals something you did not want to see.

You run your Annual Check and something comes up that you were not fully prepared for. A Major Fault in an area you had been quietly hoping was fine. The honest score is lower than you expected. The verdict is clear. And the size of what addressing it feels like threatens to shut the whole process down before you can take a single step.

Which Area of Your Life Is Most Likely to Produce This Moment?
What Is the Smallest Possible First Step When the Fault Feels Too Big?
🎯 The Reframe — Answer This

When the fault feels too large to address, what are you assuming about your capacity to handle what addressing it will require?

This is a belief, not a fact. What evidence from your own life suggests that belief is not accurate?
The Garage Principle

The car that fails its MOT does not fix itself before returning. It goes to the garage one fault at a time. You do not have to fix everything today. You have to start with one thing. The one thing is always accessible. It is the whole thing that is not.

Before You Close This

You have done something most people never do. You have stopped moving for long enough to look honestly at where you actually are. You have named the faults. Counted the cost. Traced the root. Built the system. Committed to the step.

Before you close this session — write one last thing. What is the most honest thing that happened in this worksheet? Not the most useful insight — the moment where something actually shifted.

The most successful people are not the ones with perfect lives. They are the ones who know exactly where they are — who have looked honestly, named what needs work, and have a plan. That clarity is what lets you move without the low-grade anxiety of the unexamined fault.

You are not just roadworthy. You are built for the journey. Keep living in full expression.
