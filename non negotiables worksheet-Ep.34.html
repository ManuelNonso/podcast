"A value is something you aspire to. A non-negotiable is something you will not compromise — even when it's costly, even when a good reason is given, even when someone more powerful than you is asking."

Dario Amodei didn't decide his red lines on the day the Pentagon threatened him. He said: "We've had them from day one." That's why he could say no — clearly, calmly, and without hesitation — when $200 million and a presidential threat were on the table.

This worksheet is designed to give you that same settled clarity. Not rigidity. Not stubbornness. Settledness. The kind that comes from having already decided — long before the pressure arrives.

🔍 Part 1: Discover Your Non-Negotiables

Before you can define your non-negotiables, you need to find them. They already exist inside you — this section helps you surface them through four targeted exercises.

Exercise 1 of 4

The Violation Test — What Has Already Crossed a Line?

The clearest signal of a non-negotiable is a time when someone or something crossed a line and you felt it viscerally — even if you didn't act on it. Betrayal, resentment, and deep discomfort are data points. They tell you what you actually value, not just what you say you value.

🧠 Why This Works: Your strongest non-negotiables are usually already proven by experience. You've already felt them being violated. This exercise helps you name what you've been feeling without clear language.

Think of 3 moments when you felt deeply compromised, resentful, or like you'd betrayed yourself. What happened?

💡 Examples to Prompt Your Thinking:
  • You stayed quiet in a meeting when someone took credit for your work — and resented yourself for it
  • You took on a client you knew wasn't right and it drained you for months
  • You said yes to something that required you to be dishonest — and it kept you up at night
  • You stayed in a role/relationship longer than you should have because leaving felt too costly
Exercise 2 of 4

The Deathbed Test — What Would You Regret?

This exercise is uncomfortable by design. It cuts through the noise of daily life and forces clarity about what actually matters. Answer honestly — not aspirationally.

🧠 Why This Works: Bronnie Ware, a palliative care nurse who documented the regrets of dying patients, found the most common regret was: "I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected of me." This exercise surfaces the version of you that already knows what matters most.

Imagine you are 90 years old, looking back at your life. Answer these questions:

What would you regret compromising most — in your career?

What would you regret compromising most — in your relationships?

What would you regret compromising most — about who you were as a person?

What one thing, if you protected it throughout your life, would make you feel you lived in full expression?

Exercise 3 of 4

The Admiration Test — What Do You Respect Most in Others?

The qualities you most admire in others are often the qualities you most want to embody yourself — and the ones you're most committed to not betraying. This is a fast but revealing exercise.

Think of 3 people you deeply admire — in business, history, or your personal life. What is the ONE quality in each that you admire most?

Look at the three qualities you wrote. What do they have in common? What does that tell you about what you're committed to not compromising?

⚒️ Part 2: Define Your Three Non-Negotiables

Using everything you surfaced in Part 1, you're now going to define your three core non-negotiables with precision. Not vague intentions. Specific, named commitments that you can hold yourself to under real pressure.

Before You Write

Values vs. Non-Negotiables — Know the Difference

Many people confuse values with non-negotiables. They are related but not the same. Understanding the difference is what makes this exercise powerful.

⚡ A Value
  • Something you aspire to
  • A direction you move toward
  • Can be imperfectly lived
  • Guides your decisions
  • Example: "I value honesty"
🛡️ A Non-Negotiable
  • A line you will not cross
  • Binary — you either hold it or you don't
  • Has a real cost when violated
  • Applies even when it's expensive
  • Example: "I will not mislead a client to close a sale — even if I lose the deal"
💡 The Formula: A strong non-negotiable follows this structure: "I will not [specific action] — even if [the most likely pressure you'd face]." The "even if" is what makes it real. Without it, it's just a preference.

Sharpen Your Language

The Specificity Test — Are Your Non-Negotiables Clear Enough?

A non-negotiable that is too vague will fail under pressure because you'll be able to argue yourself around it. Run each of your three through this test:

Test Question Non-Neg #1 Non-Neg #2 Non-Neg #3
Could I explain this to someone in one sentence?
Would I know immediately if I crossed it?
Does it have an "even if" — a named pressure scenario?
Does it connect to who I want to be, not just what I want to avoid?
⚠️ If you answered "Needs work" to more than one question for any non-negotiable: Go back and rewrite it. A vague non-negotiable is not a non-negotiable — it's a good intention. Good intentions bend. Precise commitments hold.
🔥 Part 3: Pressure-Test Your Non-Negotiables

Defining your non-negotiables on paper is the first step. The second step is rehearsing them under simulated pressure — so when real pressure comes, the decision is already made. This is the section most people skip. Don't skip it.

Pressure Scenario 1

The Money Test

🎯 The Scenario:

Someone offers you significantly more money — a client, an employer, a partner — and the only condition is that you cross one of your three non-negotiables. The opportunity is real. The person asking is credible. The timing is ideal. You need the money.

Which of your three non-negotiables is most at risk in this scenario?

What will you say? Write your exact response — not the concept, the actual words:

Write your response here as if the conversation is happening right now. Be specific. What do you say to them?

What is the real cost of saying yes? (Go beyond the obvious — what does it do to your identity, your trust in yourself, your long-term reputation?)

💡 Remember: Anthropic lost $200M. Within days the market rewarded their refusal with a #1 app store ranking and an explosion in trust. The short-term cost of holding the line is almost always smaller than the long-term cost of losing your integrity.
Pressure Scenario 2

The Authority Test

🎯 The Scenario:

Someone with significant authority over you — a senior partner, a major client, a boss, an investor — asks you to do something that crosses one of your non-negotiables. They are not being malicious. They believe they have a good reason. But it would require you to compromise.

Walk through your response step by step — what do you do first, second, third?

What is the story you'll tell yourself in the moment to justify crossing the line — and how will you counter it?

Pressure Scenario 3

The Gradual Erosion Test

🎯 The Scenario:

Nobody asks you to cross a line dramatically. Instead, over six months, you make a series of small concessions — each one feeling reasonable, each one slightly further from your non-negotiable. Before you know it, you've drifted so far you don't recognise where you are.

Describe what gradual erosion might look like for each of your three non-negotiables. What are the small steps that could lead to crossing the line?

What is your early warning signal for each one — the moment you'll know the drift has begun?

⚠️ The Gradual Erosion Principle: Most integrity failures are not dramatic betrayals — they are sequences of small, "reasonable" compromises. The early warning signal you just wrote is your most important protection. When you feel it, treat it like an alarm. Stop. Reassess. Recommit.
📅 Part 4: Protect Your Non-Negotiables Long-Term

Deciding your non-negotiables once is not enough. Life changes. Pressure evolves. New temptations emerge. This section gives you the ongoing structure to keep your commitments alive — not as a document you filed away, but as a living part of how you operate.

📜 Your Non-Negotiables Commitment Contract

This is not a motivational exercise. This is a written record of who you have decided to be. Sign it. Date it. Keep it somewhere visible.

Your Ongoing Audit Schedule

How to Keep Your Non-Negotiables Alive

Your non-negotiables need a review rhythm. Not because they'll change drastically — but because life will gradually test them without announcing itself. Build this into your calendar today.

🗓️ Monthly Check-In

15 minutes. Ask yourself three questions:

Have I held all three this month?
Was there a moment I came close to crossing a line?
What made it hard — and what held me?
📆 Quarterly Deep Review

45 minutes. Revisit the full worksheet:

Have any new pressure patterns emerged?
Do my non-negotiables still reflect who I'm becoming?
Do I need to add or sharpen any?
📅 Annual Recommitment

90 minutes. Full reset:

Re-do the Violation and Deathbed tests
Rewrite and re-sign the Commitment Contract
Share updated non-negotiables with your accountability partner
The Accountability Protocol

How to Share Your Non-Negotiables With Someone You Trust

Spoken commitments hold stronger than private ones. This is not optional — it is part of the process. Here is exactly how to do it.

📝 Script for Sharing With Your Accountability Partner:
  • "I've been doing some deep work on my non-negotiables — the lines I've committed not to cross in my life and work. I'd like to share them with you, not for feedback, but to make them real by saying them out loud to someone I trust."
  • "My three are: [state each one clearly with the 'even if' clause]."
  • "I'm asking you to check in with me on these once a quarter. You don't need to hold me accountable — I'll hold myself accountable. I just need you to know what I've committed to."

Final Reflection

The Full Expression Question

Before you close this worksheet, answer this one final question. Take your time with it.

If you held your three non-negotiables for the next 10 years — without exception, regardless of the pressure — who would you become? What would your life look like?

Write your answer here. Be honest and specific. This is your north star — the version of you that held the line.
